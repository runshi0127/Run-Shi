Autonomous Curling Robot
DROID — Head-to-head competitive autonomous robot
Feb 2026 – Mar 2026 Stanford, CA
Arduino Mega State Machine IR Sensors Ultrasonic Sensors Servo Motor Mechanical Design
Concept
Designed and built a fully autonomous robot to compete in a head-to-head curling match on a 16-foot arena within 3 weeks. Starting from a random position, the robot must self-orient, follow a line to the shooting point, launch 8-oz steel pucks into the scoring area, and return to base for reloading — all within a 2-minute time limit and a 13 × 13 in starting footprint.
Mechanical Design
The robot uses a two-level chassis to cleanly separate subsystems: the upper level houses the servo-driven puck dispenser, battery, and power switch, while the lower level carries the four-wheel drivetrain (12V DC motors), all IR sensors, and ultrasonic sensors.
Dispensing System: A rotating dispenser driven by a single servo stores up to three pucks on the upper platform, maximizing gravitational potential energy before release. Critically, the dispenser is mounted off-center relative to the robot's midline — this aligns the striking force with the slide's longitudinal axis, delivering a pure forward impulse. A center-mounted configuration would introduce a lateral force component, causing the puck to bounce off the guide rails and lose energy. The offset eliminates sideways momentum loss and reduces wall friction entirely.
Parametric Slide: Rather than a standard linear ramp, the slide uses a parametric curve that front-loads acceleration — the puck reaches near-maximum velocity within the first 20% of travel distance and sustains peak speed for the remainder of the path. The smooth, continuous curve prevents the puck from losing contact with the surface, and the tangential horizontal exit ensures the puck's velocity vector is purely longitudinal with no vertical bounce on release.
Subsystem Detail: Sensors are distributed for specific tasks — two pairs of ultrasonic sensors on the left and rear faces handle global orientation; a front IR pair manages boundary detection; a rear IR sensor handles reverse maneuvers; and a central triple-sensor array provides precise line following. The dispensing mechanism sits entirely on the upper platform with the servo bolted directly to the top board.
Design Evolution: An initial double-flywheel concept (two compliant wheels at high RPM) was prototyped and tested. It was ultimately removed when testing showed that simply elevating the pucks on the upper level gave the passive slide sufficient energy to reach the scoring zone — eliminating motor complexity and weight. Gate-and-slide and shoving concepts were also evaluated but rejected due to space constraints, servo torque limitations, and hog-line penalty risk.
Electrical
The electrical system is centered around an Arduino Mega 2560 as the primary controller, powered by a 7.2V NiMH battery.

Power Distribution: The 7.2V rail powers the Arduino (via Vin), the dispensing servo, and a boost converter. A step-up converter generates a stable 12V rail for the two L298N motor drivers. All IR and ultrasonic sensors draw from the Arduino's 5V output pin.

Sensor Suite: Six digital IR sensors cover all navigation tasks — a front boundary pair for hog-line detection, a rear sensor for reverse navigation, and a central triple-array for line following. Four ultrasonic sensors (arranged in two pairs on the left and rear faces) provide wall-distance feedback for global orientation. The dispensing servo is driven via a single PWM pin for the 270° rotation cycle.

Motor Drive: Two L298N H-bridge modules manage the four-wheel drivetrain, each controlled via digital direction pins and PWM speed pins.

Component Calculations: Peak current draw — four drive motors (2A total) + servo + sensors + Arduino (~1A) — totals ~7A peak; applying a 1.4× safety factor gives a 10A fuse. Servo torque requirement: worst-case 3 pucks × 8 oz each on a 3-inch arm = 72 ozf·in (5.2 kgf·cm), necessitating a high-torque 270° servo (a standard 180° servo cannot complete the full dispensing arc).

Sensor Layout Evolution: The initial design used four front IR sensors + phototransistors for IR beacon identification + four ultrasonic sensors in all cardinal directions. The final layout dropped the beacon system entirely, replaced it with two front-facing IR sensors flanking the slide for direct hog-line detection, expanded to six IR sensors total, and repositioned the ultrasonics into two rear/left pairs for parallel-to-wall verification.

Algorithm
The state machine runs four phases: (1) Corner alignment — four ultrasonic sensors (two rear, two left) rotate the robot until both pairs read equal distances, confirming alignment parallel to the walls. (2) Line acquisition — the robot advances until a rear IR sensor detects the hogline, then turns right until the front-right IR sensor picks up the center line. (3) Line following & shooting — three mid-IR sensors steer the robot (left/right correction or straight), stopping when both front IR sensors detect the shooting line; a 360° servo then dispenses all pucks over 2 seconds. (4) Return & reload — the robot reverses along the line, stops when the rear ultrasonic reads under 10 cm, rotates to face the starting zone, and waits 10 seconds before repeating the cycle.

Saxaul Seedling Auto-Planting Robot
Autonomous desert reforestation vehicle with hollow-drill seedling mechanism
Oct 2023 – Sep 2024 Shanghai, China · Tengger Desert, Gansu
SolidWorks 3D Printing Laser Cutting Arduino Mechanism Design Field Testing Team Lead
Concept
Saxaul trees are critical for combating desertification in northwestern China — they anchor soil and support the cultivation of Cistanche, a high-value medicinal crop. However, harsh desert conditions (sandstorms, wild rabbits, gerbil damage) result in low survival rates. This project designed an autonomous solar-powered trolley to patrol a planting base, replant seedlings, and return to charge automatically — enabling unattended, scalable reforestation.
Design
The vehicle integrates three core subsystems. The seedling warehouse uses a roller mechanism (stepper motor, 1:380 gear ratio) to dispense one seedling per cycle from a dual-sided acrylic tray. The transport mechanism uses a linkage-driven cylindrical tube with a gear-rack opening device and internal spring to orient and deliver the seedling upright to the drill. The hollow drill — the core innovation — is a T-thread (pitch 10 mm) alloy screw that rotates into the sand while a fish-line/steel-wire internal mechanism opens the drill tip at depth to deposit the seedling, then retracts to close. The chassis uses four independently driven soft rubber wheels with an Ackermann steering system adapted for loose sand.
Result
Conducted field testing at the Tengger Desert in Gansu during winter break. Identified and resolved drill penetration issues by adding a ball screw, widening the thread profile, and upgrading to a higher-torque stepper motor. FEA in SolidWorks SimulationXpress confirmed the drill's structural safety factor exceeded 1.19×10⁶. The final prototype successfully completed the full planting cycle: seedling dispensing, upright transport, drill descent, tip opening, seedling deposit, and drill retraction.
